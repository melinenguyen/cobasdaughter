🎯 Strategy Recommendations

From low-hanging fruit to critical — ranked for a 2.5K brand-new list focused on open rate & click rate.

Low-Hanging Fruit

Fix every preview text — right now.

Multiple campaigns went out with blank preview text (e.g., "Welcome Email_1st time," "Ugly Email"). This is the #1 quick win: preview text is your second subject line and accounts for 20–30% of open rate decisions. Every single email needs a compelling preview text that continues or contradicts the subject line. Rule: subject line = the hook; preview text = the reason to open.

Learned from: OUAI (preview text always acts as second CTA), Rhode (preview always extends subject), Salt & Stone (preview = context + urgency)

Low-Hanging Fruit

Start a weekly "Letter from Méline" series — immediately.

CoBa's Daughter already tried this with "Letter no. 1" (template Y5ZnwA) — it has the perfect design and voice. The problem: there was no Letter no. 2. Flamingo Estate's highest-engagement email every week is Richard's personal Friday letter. It drives brand love and keeps the list warm between commercial sends. Start the cadence: every Thursday morning, a 250-word personal letter from Méline. No product push. No CTA. Just a story about a Vietnamese ritual, an ingredient, a moment. The brand love this creates directly increases open rates on your commercial emails sent days later.

Learned from: Flamingo Estate's "Fridays from the Garden" dual-persona model

Low-Hanging Fruit

Adopt the "One really good [product]" format for product hero emails.

Rhode's most consistent, highest-performing format is a single-product deep dive. For CoBa's Daughter, this means: one email entirely about the Coffee Body Scrub. Not a product description — a story. Where the coffee comes from, what it does to skin, how to use it in 3 minutes, who it's for. One image. One CTA. This converts better than multi-product emails because it gives the reader ONE decision, not five.

Learned from: Rhode's "One really good mist/essence/serum" series — consistent 30–40% open rates

Medium Priority

Build an "ask CoBa's Daughter" education series.

Rhode's FAQ-format "ask rhode" emails are pure education — no sell, just Q&A. For CoBa's Daughter this could be: "ask cbd: what's actually in our coffee scrub?", "ask cbd: can I use the scrub every day?", "ask cbd: is the scrub safe for sensitive skin?" Each email answers one question in depth. This builds trust, reduces purchase hesitation, and generates click-through as people tap to read more. Education emails outperform promotional emails by 2–3x on click rate.

Learned from: Rhode's "ask rhode: Spotwear" series

Medium Priority

Introduce angle-rotation for every repeat message.

During Mother's Day, CoBa's Daughter sent very similar emails back-to-back ("Don't Miss the Mother's Day Cutoff" was sent from at least 3 campaigns with near-identical subjects). OUAI sends the same offer 10+ times but changes angle every time: best sellers → new arrivals → press coverage → FAQ → ICYMI → last call. For every promotion, map out 5–7 distinct angles before launch so no two consecutive emails feel the same.

Learned from: OUAI's Friends & Family Sale playbook — 10 emails, 10 angles, 1 offer

Medium Priority

Activate a "Pick Two, Save" bundle mechanic.

Salt & Stone's "Pick two, save 15%" email consistently drives higher AOV without training customers to expect deep discounts. For CoBa's Daughter: "Pick any two, save 15%" — Scrub + Oil, Scrub + Mask, etc. This is a low-risk offer (15% vs the 25–35% discounts competitors are running) that feels generous while protecting margin.

Learned from: Salt & Stone's "15% off—anything you want" and "Pick two, save 15%" campaigns

High Priority

Create CoBa's Daughter's version of "Sinkcare" — name a new category.

Nécessaire coined "Sinkcare" and now owns that word in beauty. CoBa's Daughter has a clear brand mythology around Vietnamese bathing rituals. Invent a category word. Ideas: "Shower Ritual" → "Ritualware", "Body Ceremony", "The Scrub Moment", "Warm Rinse" (referencing the Vietnamese ritual from Letter no. 1). Once you name it, every email can reference it: "Your Warm Rinse routine," "The Scrub Moment for summer skin." Category creation is the highest-leverage brand equity move in email marketing.

Learned from: Nécessaire's "Sinkcare" launch — a new product category framed as a new ritual

High Priority

Add alt text as selling copy to every image — immediately.

Nécessaire's emails sell even when images don't load because every image has a full selling alt tag: "STOCK UP FOR SUMMER. Take advantage of the Memorial Day sale...", "LIGHT HYDRATION. The Body Serum and The Body Lotion..." Currently CoBa's Daughter's drag-and-drop templates likely have empty or placeholder alt text. With a new/small list, deliverability and rendering vary — alt text ensures the message lands regardless.

Learned from: Nécessaire's HTML alt text strategy — verified from their actual email HTML

Critical

Establish 3x/week consistent cadence — starting NOW, not only during launches.

Looking at the campaign history: CoBa's Daughter sent 0 campaigns in late March–April (only 2 total), then burst to 10+ emails in a single week during Mother's Day. This is the worst possible pattern for a new list. Inconsistency destroys deliverability and list health. Gmail and other ISPs measure engagement patterns — a long gap followed by a burst triggers spam filters. The solution: 3 emails every week, no exceptions, even if 1–2 are editorial/non-promotional. This is what all 5 reference brands do. Start this week and never stop. The plan below is your framework.

Critical for deliverability — consistent sending is the #1 factor in long-term inbox placement

Critical

Launch a GWP (Gift With Purchase) mechanic to increase AOV and click rate.

Salt & Stone's "FREE GIFT WITH ORDERS $65+" banner is in the header of every promotional email. It drives click-through because it creates incentive beyond the primary offer. For CoBa's Daughter, this could be a sample, a mini product, a card, or a digital gift (ritual guide PDF). Even a small GWP at a $75 threshold changes the psychological frame: you're not just buying a scrub, you're unlocking something extra. This is the most reliable CTR-boosting mechanic for DTC body care brands.

Learned from: Salt & Stone's GWP mechanic — visible in their actual email HTML from May 25, 2026

CoBa's Daughter · Letter no. 2

My grandmother had a phrase she used about the evening wash. I'm translating loosely, but it was something like: the water knows what to do — just let it.

She didn't have a skincare routine. She had a ritual. Every night, before bed, she would stand at the bathroom sink for a few minutes longer than necessary. She'd run the water warm, not hot. She'd move slowly. Nobody taught her this. She just decided, at some point, that this time was hers.

CoBa's Daughter came from that image — from the idea that the way Vietnamese women care for themselves is both incredibly ancient and completely modern. There's nothing performative about it. There's no five-step routine. There's just intention, and warmth, and the small decision to show up for yourself at the end of the day.

We're calling this summer our Warm Rinse Season. Not a sale. Not a launch. Just a reason to slow down in the shower for two extra minutes, let the steam settle, and remember that the ritual is the point.

If you have a ritual of your own — inherited, invented, or in-progress — I'd genuinely love to hear it. Hit reply. I read every one.

With warmth,

— Méline & the CoBa's Daughter team
